Canvas 22 · Protocol

Dual-Rail Architecture — internal ledger first.

Sardis v1.1 inverts the usual fintech stack: the internal ledger is the primary rail, and external rails (ACH, card, stablecoin, wire, FX bridge) become on-ramp and off-ramp only. When both parties are on Sardis, a payment completes in ~1ms with $0 fee and zero external API calls. When the merchant is off-Sardis, we fall back to the cheapest external rail that matches the mandate's rail_scope. This is the PayPal model applied to agent commerce.

TL;DR Rails are a settlement surface, not a settlement strategy. Most agent payments in a mature ecosystem will be Sardis-to-Sardis — the same way most PayPal dollars never leave PayPal. External rails stay as ramps; internal ledger carries the volume.
The two rails

Primary vs. fallback.

Primary rail
Sardis internal ledger
An atomic, double-entry ledger inside Sardis. Payer's FundingCells (UTXO) debit in one row; merchant's Sardis balance credits in another. Both writes happen in a single PostgreSQL transaction with FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED on the cell row.
  • ~1ms end-to-end (database commit latency)
  • $0 marginal fee (no external API calls)
  • Full audit — every ledger row hash-chained, Merkle-batched, on-chain anchored
  • No chain reorg risk — database is final
  • Reversible via arbitration up to dispute_window expiry
Fallback rails
External settlement (when merchant is off-Sardis)
The liquidity router selects the best rail based on: merchant's preferred settlement currency, mandate's rail_scope, FX slippage budget, real-time rail health.
  • x402 — stablecoin, HTTP-native
  • Card (Stripe Connect, Adyen) — existing merchant account
  • Bank (ACH / RTP / SEPA / PIX / Wire) — direct deposit
  • FX bridge (Bridge.xyz, Wise) — cross-currency
  • MPP (Stripe Merchant Payment Protocol) — Tempo settlement
On-ramp

How money enters Sardis.

Principals fund their vaults via any rail that makes sense for their treasury. Once on Sardis, those funds become FundingCells and circulate between agents and merchants entirely inside the ledger.

Bank transfer
ACH pull, wire, SEPA, Faster Payments. Settles to principal's custodial vault in 1–3 business days (varies by rail).
Card funding
Debit card load for quick top-up. Standard card processing fees apply. Covers agents that need liquidity inside minutes.
USDC / stablecoin
On-chain deposit to a Sardis-controlled smart wallet. Near-instant, low fee. Most common for crypto-native principals.
Credit line
A credit-backed mandate draws against a pre-approved revolving line; repayment schedules as a subscription mandate back to the principal's treasury.
Stablecoin bridges
USDC.e and other variants bridge in via Circle CCTP or LayerZero; normalized to canonical USDC inside Sardis.
Smart wallet
An ERC-4337 smart account pre-authorizes Sardis as a spender; agent payments draw down from there without per-tx signatures.
Off-ramp

How money leaves Sardis.

Only when the merchant is off-Sardis. The liquidity router picks the cheapest rail that matches the merchant's preferred payout currency and the mandate's rail_scope.

Why this architecture

The PayPal parallel.

PayPal processes roughly 95% of its dollar-flow inside its own ledger. Users top up once, receive payments from other PayPal users, then off-ramp occasionally. The external rails (bank, card) are funnels, not settlement. PayPal's moat isn't the rails — it's the closed loop.
Why it matters for agent commerce
Agents transact at sub-cent granularity. Running every micro-payment through card rails (2.9%+$0.30) or ACH (min $0.25) is economically dead. The only way agent commerce scales is if ~90% of volume stays on an internal ledger where marginal cost is a database write. Sardis is built around that reality from day one.
Why external rails still matter
Merchants have suppliers, payroll, landlords — obligations that live in fiat. The off-ramp has to work every day on time. Sardis doesn't pretend the dollar doesn't exist; it just doesn't route agent-to-agent dollars through the ACH network.
